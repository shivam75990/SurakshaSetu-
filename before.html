Before a Disaster

Preparation is your greatest defense. A little planning now can make all the difference when a crisis strikes.

What to Do: Essential Steps

Build an Emergency Kit

Prepare a kit with essential supplies that can sustain you and your family for at least 72 hours.

  • Water (one gallon per person per day)
  • Non-perishable food
  • First-aid kit and prescription medications
  • Flashlight and extra batteries
  • Battery-powered radio
  • Important documents and cash

Create a Communication Plan

Decide on a meeting place and a family contact outside of your local area. Text messages often work when phone calls don't.

  • Designate an out-of-state contact
  • Establish a primary and secondary meeting place
  • Make a list of emergency numbers
  • Include important personal information for each family member

Secure Your Home

Take steps to protect your property and mitigate damage from potential hazards.

  • Secure heavy furniture to walls
  • Install smoke and carbon monoxide alarms
  • Locate utility shut-off valves (gas, water, electricity)
  • Trim trees and remove dead limbs near your home

Stay Informed

Be aware of potential threats and listen to guidance from local authorities.

  • Sign up for local emergency alerts
  • Know your community's evacuation routes
  • Follow official weather and news sources
  • Learn first-aid and CPR

What NOT to Do: Common Mistakes to Avoid

Don't Wait for a Warning

Disasters can strike without warning. Waiting until a formal alert is issued can put you and your family in a dangerous situation. Always have a basic level of preparedness in place.

Don't Forget Pets or Special Needs

Your plan should include all members of your family, including pets and those with special needs. Ensure you have supplies for them and a clear plan for their safety.

Don't Rely Solely on a Single Power Source

Power outages are common during and after disasters. Have backup power for essentials like cell phones and lights.

Don't Ignore Evacuation Orders

Official evacuation orders are given for your safety. Refusing to evacuate can put you and first responders at risk.
